The Solar System:

The Formation:

The solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago when a massive, rotating cloud of interstellar gas and dust—known as the solar nebula—collapsed under its own gravity. This collapse likely triggered by a nearby exploding star (supernova) condensed most of the material into the Sun, while the rest flattened into a disk that built the planets.

The Planets:

Our solar system consists of the Sun and eight officially recognized planets, which are divided into two distinct categories: the inner rocky terrestrial planets and the outer gaseous and icy giant planets. The inner rocky planets are Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. Outer gaseous planets are Saturn and Jupiter. And outer icy giants are Uranus and Neptune.

The Sun:

The Sun is the star at the center of our Solar System. It is a huge ball of hot gases, mainly hydrogen and helium. The Sun provides light and heat, making life on Earth possible. It is about 4.6 billion years old and contains more than 99% of the Solar System's total mass. The Sun's gravity keeps all the planets, moons, asteroids, and comets orbiting around it. The surface of the Sun is extremely hot, reaching about 5,500°C (9,932°F), while its core is much hotter at around 15 million°C (27 million°F).

The Asteroid Belt:

The Asteroid Belt is a region of space located between the planets Mars and Jupiter. It contains millions of rocky objects called asteroids that orbit the Sun. These asteroids range in size from tiny rocks to large bodies hundreds of kilometers wide. Scientists believe the Asteroid Belt is made of material left over from the formation of the Solar System about 4.6 billion years ago. The largest object in the Asteroid Belt is Ceres, which is classified as a dwarf planet. Although the belt contains many asteroids, they are spread far apart, so collisions are rare.
